3. How do we decide the the avg_confidence? Should our DEs do the calculation? If so, based on what?

Who owns avg_confidence and how to calculate it
Short answer: Yes, your Data Engineers should own the calculation, but the rubric should be co-designed with the ESG/Audit team. Confidence is a data-trust score, not a data-science score — it has to be defensible to an auditor, not just statistically clean.

What your run tells us
Only 2 datasets currently carry a real value:

real_emissions: 87.3
real_esg_metrics: 87.6
The other 18 show avg_confidence: 0 — meaning the field exists but nothing is populating it. So this isn't a methodology question yet; it's a "pick a formula and backfill" question.

A defensible rubric (5 components, weighted)
This is the standard pattern for ESG/audit contexts. Each dataset gets scored 0–100 on five dimensions, then weighted-averaged.

Component	Weight	What DEs measure
Source reliability	25%	Tier 1 (metered/IoT/ERP) = 100, Tier 2 (invoiced/billed) = 80, Tier 3 (supplier-reported) = 60, Tier 4 (estimated/proxy) = 40
Completeness	20%	% non-null across required fields (you already compute this — it's 100% everywhere)
Timeliness / freshness	15%	Days since last_sync vs. expected cadence. <1 cycle = 100, stale = decays
Validation pass rate	20%	% of records passing schema + business rules (ranges, units, referential integrity)
Lineage & auditability	20%	Has source ID, timestamp, transformation log, owner? Binary checks summed
A record scoring 100/100/100/100/100 = avg_confidence 100. The dataset's avg_confidence is the mean across records (or a weighted mean by materiality).

Why your emissions dataset hits 87.3 (worked example)
Plausible decomposition:

Source: 90 (mix of IoT meters + invoiced energy)
Completeness: 100
Timeliness: 85 (some monthly lags)
Validation: 80 (a few unit/range flags)
Lineage: 80 (good but not every record has full transformation log)
→ Weighted = 87.0-ish. That's the kind of math an auditor can follow.

Division of labor
Data Engineers own:

Implementing the 5 component calculations as code in the pipeline
Writing it back to the avg_confidence field per dataset
Surfacing per-record confidence so it can be aggregated
ESG/Compliance team owns:

The source tier mapping (is EcoVadis Tier 2 or Tier 3 for you?)
The validation rules (what's a valid Scope 1 range for a facility?)
The weights (your run weights all 5 evenly via the 68.6 score — you may want to weight Source higher for SOX/SEC purposes)
Internal Audit signs off:

That the rubric meets ICFR-over-ESG standards (relevant given your SOX-404 gap)
Concrete next steps for your pipeline
Codify the rubric in a single function compute_confidence(dataset) — one source of truth.
Backfill the 18 datasets at 0 — even a v1 score is better than null. Your connector_iot (1,680 records, streaming) should land high; supplier_portal (0 records) should land near zero and trigger an alert, not a silent pass.
Tie the threshold to audit_ready — currently the cutoff appears to be ~87 (only 87.3 and 87.6 pass). Make this explicit: e.g., ≥85 = audit-ready, 70–84 = review, <70 = remediate.
Log component scores, not just the average. When real_supply_chain lands at 72, you need to know why — is it source tier or validation? — so DEs know what to fix.
One caution
Don't let DEs set the source tier weights alone. That's where auditors push back hardest ("why is supplier-reported scored 60 and not 40?"). Get that locked in writing with ESG + Audit before you publish numbers externally.



4. So, just by adding the avg_confidence in each dataset I can make the compliance s fall in place?

No — avg_confidence alone won't move compliance. They're two different problems.
I want to correct something from my earlier answer where I conflated these. Let me separate them cleanly.

Two independent problems in your run
Problem	What it measures	Current state	Fix
IQS / data quality	Can we trust the data?	18/20 datasets at Medium because avg_confidence is null	Populate avg_confidence
Framework compliance	Does the data answer the regulation's questions?	All frameworks at 0.0%	Build field-to-requirement mappings
Adding avg_confidence improves the first row only. Compliance % stays at 0.0% until the mapping layer is built.

Why compliance is actually at 0%
Look at the gap reasons in your tracker — they fall into two buckets:

Bucket A: "Required data not found in available metrics"

BRSR-E1 needs fields named emissions_scope1, emissions_scope2
Your real_emissions dataset has 10,080 records but the mapper can't find those exact field names
→ This is a schema/mapping problem, not a data problem or a confidence problem
Bucket B: "No data mapping available"

CSRD-DM needs materiality_assessment
You have real_materiality_assessment with 25,776 records
The mapper has no rule connecting them
→ Pure mapping config gap
Neither bucket is solved by confidence scores.

The correct sequence
Build the framework mapper — a config/lookup that says:

BRSR-E1.emissions_scope1 → real_emissions.scope1_tco2e (or whatever your column is called)
CSRD-DM.materiality_assessment → real_materiality_assessment
SOX-404.icfr_assessment → real_icfr_controls
…etc for all 57 requirements across the 6 frameworks
This is what moves compliance from 0% to something real.

Then populate avg_confidence — this determines whether the now-mapped data is audit-ready (currently only 2 datasets clear that bar).

Then the audit-ready compliance % — the meaningful number for executives — becomes: requirements covered by mapped AND audit-ready data.

Analogy
Mapping = connecting the pipes between your data and the regulation's questions.
avg_confidence = certifying the water in those pipes is drinkable.
If the pipes aren't connected, certifying the water doesn't help anyone.

What to tell your team
DEs: own avg_confidence calculation (the rubric we discussed).
ESG/Compliance analyst (or whoever owns the regulatory_tracker): owns the framework mapping config — this is the higher-leverage fix and should run in parallel, not after.
Both must land before you can claim any non-zero audit-ready compliance %.
Do the mapping first if you have to choose — it unlocks visible compliance movement. Confidence scoring upgrades that movement from "covered" to "covered and defensible."
